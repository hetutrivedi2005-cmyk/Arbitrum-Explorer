Web2 vs Web3

Web2 represents the current read-write internet dominated by centralized platforms. Web3 introduces the read-write-own model powered by cryptographic networks.

Feature Web2 (Centralized Web) Web3 (Decentralized Web)
Ownership Companies own user data, content, and code. Users own their digital assets, identities, and data.
Control Centralized servers (AWS, Google, Meta). Distributed node networks run by validators.
Login OAuth (Sign in with Google) or Email/Password. Cryptographic wallet keypairs (Sign with MetaMask).
Payments Fiat rails (Stripe, PayPal, wire transfers). Smart contracts processing digital tokens (ETH, stablecoins).
Storage Cloud giants (Amazon S3, Google Cloud). Distributed storage networks (IPFS, Filecoin, Arweave).
Transparency Closed-source databases, proprietary algorithms. Open-source smart contracts, auditable public ledger.
Web3 Advantages
  • Censorship-resistant; content cannot be unilaterally deleted.
  • Permissionless logins; no centralized platform can ban you.
  • Programmable value and money natively integrated into websites.
Web3 Drawbacks
  • Higher complexity; users must protect their own key phrases.
  • Scalability issues; base L1 layer is slower and expensive.
  • Irreversible transactions; no customer support if you make a mistake.

Did You Know?

The term "Web3" was coined in 2014 by Gavin Wood, co-founder of Ethereum and creator of Polkadot, to describe an internet centered around decentralized trust.

Bitcoin vs Ethereum

While both are leading cryptographic networks, Bitcoin was engineered as a decentralized monetary system, whereas Ethereum serves as a decentralized software computer.

Parameter Bitcoin (BTC) Ethereum (ETH)
Purpose Store of value, digital gold, peer-to-peer cash. Global smart contract executor, dApp platform.
Supply Hard cap of 21 Million coins. Deflationary. No absolute cap; dynamic issuance based on usage burn.
Smart Contracts Extremely limited (Script, non-Turing complete). Full Turing-complete smart contracts via EVM.
Speed ~7 Transactions/sec. Blocks mined every 10 mins. ~15 Transactions/sec. Blocks resolved in ~12 seconds.
Consensus Proof of Work (PoW) - ASIC hardware mining. Proof of Stake (PoS) - Staking validators.
Use Cases Inflation hedge, payments, reserve asset. DeFi, NFTs, Layer 2 networks, decentralized identity.

Why Layer 2 networks build on Ethereum, not Bitcoin

Ethereum's native Smart Contracts (Turing completeness) allow developers to write state-locking scripts. This enables L2s to securely prove off-chain calculations back on the L1 mainnet.

Public Key vs Private Key

Web3 relies on asymmetric cryptography. You share your public address to receive assets, but use your private key to prove ownership and authorize actions.

Metric Public Key (Address) Private Key (Seed Phrase)
Meaning Your cryptographic address, similar to an IBAN/email. Your account signature passcode, similar to a banking PIN.
Visibility Publicly visible. Safe to share with anyone. Strictly secret. Never share it under any circumstance.
Usage Used to route assets to your wallet. Used to sign digital messages and authorize spend requests.
Security Sharing poses no security threat. Loss of private key = absolute loss of all funds.
Examples 0x4facfe...00f2fe A 12-word seed phrase: apple banana cherry...
CRITICAL WARNING

There is no "Forgot Password" in Web3. If you lose your private key or seed phrase, you lose access to your wallet forever. Anyone who gets access to your private key can steal all of your assets. Keep it written down offline, and never enter it into unverified websites.

Optimistic vs Zero-Knowledge (ZK) Rollups

Optimistic rollups (like Arbitrum) assume transactions are valid by default and provide a challenge period. ZK rollups generate mathematical cryptographic proofs (SNARKs) to prove validity instantly.

Blockchain vs Database

Both databases and blockchains record transactions and state information, but they solve entirely different problems regarding centralized authority, write permissions, and trust.

Dimension Traditional Database (SQL / NoSQL) Blockchain (e.g. Arbitrum, Ethereum)
Ownership Controlled by a single organization or system admin. Shared across a global network of independent nodes.
Security Access control lists, firewalls, and server credentials. Cryptographic verification, consensus protocols.
Transparency Private logs accessible only to internal personnel. Publicly viewable block explorer and transaction record.
Editing Full CRUD support: Create, Read, Update, Delete. Append-only logs. Data cannot be deleted or modified.
Trust Users must trust the system administrator. Trustless. Mathematics and cryptography enforce rules.
Examples PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Oracle, MySQL. Arbitrum, Ethereum, Bitcoin, Solana.

Optimistic Rollups vs Zero-Knowledge (ZK) Rollups

Optimistic and ZK-Rollups are the two dominant architectures for scaling Ethereum L2. While both bundle transactions off-chain, their validation proofs use completely different logic.

Metric Optimistic Rollups (e.g. Arbitrum, Optimism) Zero-Knowledge Rollups (e.g. zkSync, Starknet)
Validity Proofs None posted on-chain by default. Assumed valid unless challenged. Mathematical validity proofs (SNARKs/STARKs) posted with every batch.
Dispute Mechanism Fraud Proofs. Requires a 7-day challenge window. No disputes. Cryptographic math proves execution correctness.
Withdrawal Finality Takes ~7 days due to the dispute challenge window. Near-instant (takes only as long to generate/verify proof, ~1 hour).
Throughput High. (Up to 40,000 TPS). Extremely high. (Calculations are compressed mathematically).
EVM Compatibility Excellent. Almost 100% bytecode identical to Ethereum L1. Difficult. Requires complex ZK compilers (solidity to custom assembly).
Cost Extremely cheap (Simple execution logs posted to L1). Slightly higher L2 computation costs (generating proofs is heavy).
Optimistic Rollup (Arbitrum) Edge
  • Instantly compatible with all Ethereum toolings (Hardhat, Foundry, Remix).
  • Lower processing costs for simple transactions.
  • Battle-tested in production with billions of dollars locked.
ZK-Rollup Edge
  • Instant withdraws from L2 to L1 (no 7-day waiting periods).
  • Mathematically proven security that doesn't rely on validators challenging fraud.
  • Better for enterprise transfers and massive batch scaling.

What are Ethereum Blobs (EIP-4844)?

EIP-4844 introduced temporary data packets called "Blobs" to Ethereum L1. Instead of storing rollup transaction data forever on L1 (which is very expensive), blobs discard the data after 18 days, reducing Arbitrum gas fees by over 90%!

Web3 Scaling & Cryptography Quiz

Test your understanding of the Web3 scaling concepts, asymmetric cryptography, and rollups. Select one option for each question and verify your score.

1. What does an Optimistic Rollup (such as Arbitrum) do?

Translates smart contracts from Solidity to Python code.
Bundles transactions off-chain and submits them to Ethereum L1, assuming validity by default.
Deletes historic block state from mainnet to reduce local hard drive footprint.
Correct! Rollups bundle thousands of transactions off-chain, compressing data before posting it to L1. They are "Optimistic" because they assume transactions are valid unless challenged by a node.

2. Which cryptographic key should you NEVER share under any circumstance?

Your Public Wallet Key Address (used to receive funds).
Your Private key / 12-word seed phrase (passcode to sign and spend).
Your custom dApp interface name tag.
Correct! Your Private Key proves absolute ownership of address assets. If leaked, your wallet can be completely drained without any recovery option.

3. What is the role of a Nonce in Proof of Work mining?

A static transaction fee multiplier token.
An arbitrary number altered by miners to change the output hash, matching the zero-prefix difficulty.
An identity name tag to login to MetaMask.
Correct! Miners continuously iterate the Nonce value. Since the input data is fixed, changing the Nonce changes the SHA-256 output, allowing miners to look for a hash that satisfies the leading-zero difficulty.
