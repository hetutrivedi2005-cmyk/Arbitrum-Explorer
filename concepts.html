Web2 vs Web3

Web2 represents the current read-write internet dominated by centralized platforms. Web3 introduces the read-write-own model powered by cryptographic networks.

Key Takeaway

Web2 centralized power and profits in large platforms. Web3 uses decentralized ledgers to give users direct digital ownership and control over their accounts, value, and code.

Real-World Example

In Web2, you login to Spotify using your Google profile (OAuth), meaning Google controls your identity database. In Web3, you connect to Uniswap using your MetaMask private key, which is globally recognized without requiring anyone's permission.

Feature Web2 (Centralized Web) Web3 (Decentralized Web)
Ownership Companies own user data, content, and code. Users own their digital assets, identities, and data.
Control Centralized servers (AWS, Google, Meta). Distributed node networks run by validators.
Login OAuth (Sign in with Google) or Email/Password. Cryptographic wallet keypairs (Sign with MetaMask).
Payments Fiat rails (Stripe, PayPal, wire transfers). Smart contracts processing digital tokens (ETH, stablecoins).
Storage Cloud giants (Amazon S3, Google Cloud). Distributed storage networks (IPFS, Filecoin, Arweave).
Transparency Closed-source databases, proprietary algorithms. Open-source smart contracts, auditable public ledger.
Web3 Advantages
  • Censorship-resistant; content cannot be unilaterally deleted.
  • Permissionless logins; no centralized platform can ban you.
  • Programmable value and money natively integrated into websites.
Web3 Drawbacks
  • Higher complexity; users must protect their own key phrases.
  • Scalability issues; base L1 layer is slower and expensive.
  • Irreversible transactions; no customer support if you make a mistake.
Deep Dive: Smart Contracts Explained
Smart contracts are self-executing agreements with the terms of the agreement directly written into lines of code. They run on decentralized blockchain networks like Ethereum and Arbitrum. Once deployed, they are immutable and run exactly as written, without requiring any middleman, escrow, or centralized authority. They form the foundation of decentralized finance (DeFi), automated market makers, and NFTs.

Did You Know?

The term "Web3" was coined in 2014 by Gavin Wood, co-founder of Ethereum and creator of Polkadot, to describe an internet centered around decentralized trust.

Bitcoin vs Ethereum

While both are leading cryptographic networks, Bitcoin was engineered as a decentralized monetary system, whereas Ethereum serves as a decentralized software computer.

Key Takeaway

Bitcoin acts as a passive ledger storing scarce wealth (digital gold) with very simple scripting rules. Ethereum acts as a dynamic state machine executing complex smart contracts and running decentralized applications.

Real-World Example

You use Bitcoin like a checking account or vault to hold value securely for years. You use Ethereum like an operating system, interacting with smart contracts to borrow tokens, play games, or execute automated logic.

Parameter Bitcoin (BTC) Ethereum (ETH)
Purpose Store of value, digital gold, peer-to-peer cash. Global smart contract executor, dApp platform.
Supply Hard cap of 21 Million coins. Deflationary. No absolute cap; dynamic issuance based on usage burn.
Smart Contracts Extremely limited (Script, non-Turing complete). Full Turing-complete smart contracts via EVM.
Speed ~7 Transactions/sec. Blocks mined every 10 mins. ~15 Transactions/sec. Blocks resolved in ~12 seconds.
Consensus Proof of Work (PoW) - ASIC hardware mining. Proof of Stake (PoS) - Staking validators.
Use Cases Inflation hedge, payments, reserve asset. DeFi, NFTs, Layer 2 networks, decentralized identity.
Ethereum Scalability Advantages
  • Supports layered architectures (L2s like Arbitrum) to scale to thousands of TPS.
  • Rich ecosystem of developer tools, languages (Solidity/Vyper), and nodes.
  • Transitioned to environmentally friendly Proof of Stake consensus.
Bitcoin Limitations
  • Unsuitable for building complex applications due to lack of VM states.
  • Scaling via L2 (Lightning Network) is limited to simple payments.
  • Slow transaction confirmation cycle of 10 to 60 minutes.
Deep Dive: The EVM (Ethereum Virtual Machine)
The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) is a decentralized sandboxed runtime environment that compiles and executes smart contract instructions across all nodes in the network. Every node runs EVM bytecode to keep the ledger's state synchronized. Because all computation is duplicated, EVM gas fees prevent infinity loops by charging "gas" for every operation. Layer 2 networks like Arbitrum run virtual machines (AVM/EVM) off-chain to reduce these execution costs drastically.

Why Layer 2 networks build on Ethereum, not Bitcoin

Ethereum's native Smart Contracts (Turing completeness) allow developers to write state-locking scripts. This enables L2s to securely prove off-chain calculations back on the L1 mainnet.

Public Key vs Private Key

Web3 relies on asymmetric cryptography. You share your public address to receive assets, but use your private key to prove ownership and authorize actions.

Key Takeaway

Asymmetric cryptography separates public identification from private authorization. Your public address is visible to everyone, while your private key remains a strictly guarded secret to sign smart contract interactions.

Real-World Example

Think of your public address as an email address (anyone can see it and send you emails). Think of your private key as the password to your inbox (only you can use it to log in and send outgoing emails).

Metric Public Key (Address) Private Key (Seed Phrase)
Meaning Your cryptographic address, similar to an IBAN/email. Your account signature passcode, similar to a banking PIN.
Visibility Publicly visible. Safe to share with anyone. Strictly secret. Never share it under any circumstance.
Usage Used to route assets to your wallet. Used to sign digital messages and authorize spend requests.
Security Sharing poses no security threat. Loss of private key = absolute loss of all funds.
Examples 0x4facfe...00f2fe A 12-word seed phrase: apple banana cherry...
Security Advantages
  • Cryptographic mathematical proof prevents impersonation or forgery.
  • Independence: No central database holding passwords that could get hacked.
  • Universal applicability: Same signature works on dApps across Web3.
Custody Disadvantages
  • Zero recovery features: No customer support can reset your private key.
  • Seed phrase management requires careful offline physical backups.
  • Highly vulnerable to phishing attacks requesting wallet access.
CRITICAL WARNING

There is no "Forgot Password" in Web3. If you lose your private key or seed phrase, you lose access to your wallet forever. Anyone who gets access to your private key can steal all of your assets. Keep it written down offline, and never enter it into unverified websites.

Deep Dive: How Asymmetric Signatures Work
In asymmetric cryptography, the private key is a randomly generated 256-bit number. The public key is mathematically derived from it using Elliptic Curve Cryptography (secp256k1 in Bitcoin and Ethereum). While generating a public key from a private key is fast, reversing it is mathematically impossible. A digital signature is generated by blending the message hash with the private key. Validators verify that the signature could only have been signed by the owner of the matching public key address.

Optimistic vs Zero-Knowledge (ZK) Rollups

Optimistic rollups (like Arbitrum) assume transactions are valid by default and provide a challenge period. ZK rollups generate mathematical cryptographic proofs (SNARKs) to prove validity instantly.

Blockchain vs Database

Both databases and blockchains record transactions and state information, but they solve entirely different problems regarding centralized authority, write permissions, and trust.

Key Takeaway

Traditional databases are centralized, fast, and permit CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete) edits by the system administrator. Blockchains are decentralized, append-only, immutable ledgers where state updates require cryptographic consensus.

Real-World Example

A traditional database is like a bank ledger managed by SQL tables (an engineer can manual-override or delete an entry). A blockchain is like Etherscan (where transaction logs are permanent, tamper-proof, and can never be deleted).

Dimension Traditional Database (SQL / NoSQL) Blockchain (e.g. Arbitrum, Ethereum)
Ownership Controlled by a single organization or system admin. Shared across a global network of independent nodes.
Security Access control lists, firewalls, and server credentials. Cryptographic verification, consensus protocols.
Transparency Private logs accessible only to internal personnel. Publicly viewable block explorer and transaction record.
Editing Full CRUD support: Create, Read, Update, Delete. Append-only logs. Data cannot be deleted or modified.
Trust Users must trust the system administrator. Trustless. Mathematics and cryptography enforce rules.
Examples PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Oracle, MySQL. Arbitrum, Ethereum, Bitcoin, Solana.
Blockchain Advantages
  • Immutable: Historical transaction records can never be maliciously modified.
  • Zero single points of failure; resilient against data corruption or shutdowns.
  • Publicly verifiable audit trail that establishes trustless cooperation.
Database Advantages
  • Speed: Milliseconds write speed compared to blockchain block times.
  • Storage efficiency: Can hold terabytes of media files at extremely low cost.
  • Simple recovery: Central authorities can reset access keys or edit errors.
Deep Dive: When to use a Blockchain vs Database
You should use a traditional database if your project requires high-frequency data throughput, private editing capabilities, or handles heavy media files (like social media profiles or video hosting). You should use a blockchain if you need to coordinate value and agreements between parties who do not trust each other, require strict double-spend protection for digital assets, or want to deploy censorship-resistant smart contracts.

Optimistic Rollups vs Zero-Knowledge (ZK) Rollups

Optimistic and ZK-Rollups are the two dominant architectures for scaling Ethereum L2. While both bundle transactions off-chain, their validation proofs use completely different logic.

Key Takeaway

Optimistic rollups assume all transactions are valid by default and use fraud proofs during a 7-day challenge period to catch errors. ZK-rollups mathematically prove validity instantly by posting cryptographic validity proofs (SNARKs/STARKs) directly to L1.

Real-World Example

Arbitrum is an Optimistic Rollup (fees are extremely low and L1 tooling is identical, but withdrawing back to Ethereum takes 7 days). zkSync Era is a ZK-Rollup (proof calculation requires high L2 math execution overhead, but withdrawing back to L1 takes under an hour).

Metric Optimistic Rollups (e.g. Arbitrum, Optimism) Zero-Knowledge Rollups (e.g. zkSync, Starknet)
Validity Proofs None posted on-chain by default. Assumed valid unless challenged. Mathematical validity proofs (SNARKs/STARKs) posted with every batch.
Dispute Mechanism Fraud Proofs. Requires a 7-day challenge window. No disputes. Cryptographic math proves execution correctness.
Withdrawal Finality Takes ~7 days due to the dispute challenge window. Near-instant (takes only as long to generate/verify proof, ~1 hour).
Throughput High. (Up to 40,000 TPS). Extremely high. (Calculations are compressed mathematically).
EVM Compatibility Excellent. Almost 100% bytecode identical to Ethereum L1. Difficult. Requires complex ZK compilers (solidity to custom assembly).
Cost Extremely cheap (Simple execution logs posted to L1). Slightly higher L2 computation costs (generating proofs is heavy).
Optimistic Rollup (Arbitrum) Edge
  • Instantly compatible with all Ethereum toolings (Hardhat, Foundry, Remix).
  • Lower processing costs for simple transactions.
  • Battle-tested in production with billions of dollars locked.
ZK-Rollup Edge
  • Instant withdraws from L2 to L1 (no 7-day waiting periods).
  • Mathematically proven security that doesn't rely on validators challenging fraud.
  • Better for enterprise transfers and massive batch scaling.
Deep Dive: Understanding Fraud Proof Challenges
Optimistic rollups rely on an interactive dispute protocol. When a batch is posted, validators monitor its execution states. If a validator detects an incorrect transaction, they issue a challenge on Ethereum L1. The rollup virtual machine executes the disputed step inside a specialized referee contract on L1 (interactive fraud proof). If fraud is proven, the dishonest validator's stake is slashed and the ledger states are rolled back. This dispute window is why withdrawals require a 7-day delay.

What are Ethereum Blobs (EIP-4844)?

EIP-4844 introduced temporary data packets called "Blobs" to Ethereum L1. Instead of storing rollup transaction data forever on L1 (which is very expensive), blobs discard the data after 18 days, reducing Arbitrum gas fees by over 90%!
